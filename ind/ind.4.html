Michelangelo Buonarroti, Sistine Chapel, Ceiling, Lunette: Aminadab, c. 1508-1512, Vatican City, Europe, [more]

After the Jews departed Egypt, pursued by the Egyptian army, they arrived at the shores of the Red Sea and were faced with a choice between death by the Egyptians or by sea. A man by the name of Nachshon ben (son of) Aminadab took a leap of faith, began wading into the sea, and at the very moment before drowning God caused the sea to split enabling the Jews to travel to safety. The sages of the Talmud credit Nachshon’s spirituality to his father Aminadab’s education. In contrast to most pieces painted by Michelangelo, Aminadab is painted facing forward, indicating great importance. Aminadab is subtly depicted as making “devil’s horns” with his fingers. The location of this painting, above the papal throne, creates a dynamic where Aminadabs fingers are pointing down towards where Julius would sit. Recent cleaning of the ceiling has also come to reveal Aminadab wearing a badge of shame that Jews were forced to wear at all times by the Fourth Lateran Council. This appears to be a daring sign of criticism that Michelangelo is directing at the sixteenth-century Papal Court, for treating the current descendants of the Ancestors of Jesus so badly. The criticism is subtle enough to not be spotted easily, considering that it is 65 feet up in the air, and above the papal canopy 20 .
